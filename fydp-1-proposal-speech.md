# FYDP-I Proposal Presentation Speech

## Slide 1
Good morning sir. Today I will present my FYDP-I research proposal. My topic is solving the Lorenz-1960 ODE system using an artificial neural network, or ANN, and identifying the most suitable architecture. In simple words, I want to check whether a normal ANN can learn the behavior of this system accurately. I also want to find out which ANN design performs best when we compare it with trusted numerical methods and PINN-style reference approaches. So this proposal is mainly about building a strong foundation for that comparison and making sure the future ANN study is based on reliable evidence.

## Slide 2
First, some background. Ordinary differential equations, or ODEs, are mathematical models that describe how a system changes over time. They are used in many important areas such as weather prediction, fluid motion, engineering control systems, and other dynamic physical processes. The Lorenz-1960 system is a classic example in this area. Even though it is a small system, it is nonlinear and sensitive, which means its behavior can become complicated very quickly. Because of this, it is often used as a benchmark problem. For my research, this makes it a very useful test case, because it is simple enough to study carefully, but difficult enough to make the comparison meaningful.

## Slide 3
Now I will explain the motivation behind this work. Classical numerical methods, such as Runge-Kutta, are well established and usually very accurate. However, every time we want a new result, we need to solve the system again step by step. That works well, but it can be less flexible when we want repeated evaluations or future extensions. On the other hand, if an ANN is trained properly, it may learn the solution pattern and give predictions much faster after training. That is why ANN is interesting here. My motivation is not to replace numerical methods immediately, but to study whether a plain ANN can become a practical alternative in this benchmark problem and under what conditions it performs well.

## Slide 4
The expected contribution of this work has several parts. First, I want to build a clean and problem-focused benchmark centered on the Lorenz-1960 system, instead of doing only a general ANN demonstration. Second, I want to study how important ANN design choices, such as depth, width, activation function, and training strategy, affect the final solution quality. Third, I want to create a fair comparison framework where numerical solvers, plain ANN models, and PINN-style approaches can be evaluated under the same problem setting. Finally, I want to prepare a reusable workflow for implementation, validation, and later experiments. This will make the work more organized, reproducible, and useful for the next phase of the project.

## Slide 5
This research is novel not because ANN-based solvers are completely new, but because of the specific gap I want to address. Existing studies have already explored areas like DeepONet-style operator learning, ANN methods for differential equations, and Neural ODEs. So the idea of using neural networks for such problems is already present in the literature. However, what is still not clearly answered is this: for the Lorenz-1960 benchmark, which plain ANN architecture works best when everything is tested in one unified and fair setup? That is the gap I am focusing on. This matters because without architecture-level comparison, it is difficult to know when a simple ANN is enough and when a more advanced neural method is actually necessary.

## Slide 6
This is also a complex engineering problem, not just a simple coding task. It combines mathematical modeling, numerical accuracy, model validation, ANN design, and evidence-based comparison. The Lorenz system is nonlinear and coupled, so even a small implementation mistake can affect all three state variables and reduce trust in the result. Also, before evaluating any ANN, I need a validated numerical baseline, otherwise the comparison will not be strong. On top of that, ANN performance depends on many design factors, such as depth, width, activation function, optimizer choice, and training stability. So the real challenge is to balance several things at once: accuracy, computational cost, reproducibility, and clarity of comparison.

## Slide 7
Finally, this slide explains the scope of FYDP-I. In this phase, I will first implement the Lorenz-1960 equations using a Python ODE solver library. After that, I will also implement a custom Runge-Kutta baseline so that I have a second trusted numerical reference. Then I will optimize and validate these numerical outputs carefully, because they will be used later for ANN comparison. The main deliverables of FYDP-I are a literature review matrix with gap analysis, verified numerical solution plots, reusable solver code, and baseline evaluation notes. What is not included in FYDP-I is the final ANN architecture optimization, the full ANN versus PINN experiment set, and the final recommendation across all solver types. Those parts will come in the next phase after the baseline is fully ready.

## Closing
Thank you sir. That is the overview of my FYDP-I proposal. My main goal in this phase is to build a reliable numerical foundation first, so that the later ANN comparison is accurate and meaningful. I welcome your feedback, suggestions, and any recommendations for improving the scope or direction of this work.
