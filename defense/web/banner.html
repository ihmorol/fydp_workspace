Introduction & Motivation

⚡

Classical Solvers

Highly accurate but strictly mesh-dependent. Re-integrates step-by-step for every query, causing large computational overhead.

🧠

Neural-ODE Methods

Maps inputs continuously in one shot, but typically depends on physics-informed losses or complex operator learning.

🎯

Plain-ANN Approach

Can a plain feedforward ANN (no physics in the loss) approximate the Lorenz-1960 solution purely from numerical data?

Central Question: Can a plain feedforward ANN approximate the Lorenz-1960 solution, and which architecture performs best?

Objectives

01

Dual-Solver Reference

Build a trusted reference with RK4 and SciPy DOP853 solvers, cross-validating accuracy.

02

Systematic Search

Systematically vary depth, width, and activation functions for plain ANNs (no physics aids).

03

Performance Scoring

Score performance vs cost: evaluate MSE, MAE, max error, and training/inference time.

04

PINN Comparison

Benchmark the best plain ANN configuration against published PINN / DeepONet results.

Background: The System

ẋ = −0.10 y z
ẏ = +1.60 x z
ż = −0.75 x y
k = 2, l = 1  ·  x₀=0.5, y₀=0.75, z₀=1  ·  t ∈ [0, 1]

Three coupled nonlinear ODEs — compact, but a genuinely demanding benchmark for any solver.

Bounded, quasi-periodic orbits over t ∈ [0, 50].

Research Gap & Questions

THE GAP

Prior work lacks a systematic study of plain-ANN capabilities on Lorenz-1960; literature fixes architecture, uses simpler ODEs, or relies on PINNs.

  • No architecture study for Lorenz-1960
  • No PINN vs plain-ANN comparison
  • Depth & width study is rarely combined
OUR QUESTIONS

Can plain neural networks learn coupled chaotic ODE dynamics without physical guidance, and what is the optimal design?

  • Can a plain ANN learn the solution from data alone?
  • Which configuration wins the accuracy-cost trade-off?
  • How close does it get to physics-informed baselines?

= 9 runs

69 controlled experiments — splitting the phases cleanly separates architecture effects from optimizer effects.
